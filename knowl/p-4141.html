In general, if \(f\) is any continuous function, and we define the function \(A\) by the rule

\begin{equation*} A(x) = \int_c^x f(t) \, dt\text{,} \end{equation*}

where \(c\) is an arbitrary constant, then we can show that \(A\) is an antiderivative of \(f\text{.}\) To see why, let's demonstrate that \(A'(x) = f(x)\) by using the limit definition of the derivative. Doing so, we observe that

\begin{align} A'(x) \amp = \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{A(x+h) - A(x)}{h}\notag\\ \amp = \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{\int_c^{x+h} f(t) \, dt - \int_c^x f(t) \, dt}{h}\notag\\ \amp = \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{\int_x^{x+h} f(t) \, dt}{h}\text{,}\tag{5.2.1} \end{align}

where Equation (5.2.1) follows from the fact that \(\int_c^x f(t) \,dt + \int_x^{x+h} f(t) \, dt = \int_c^{x+h} f(t) \, dt\text{.}\) Now, observe that for small values of \(h\text{,}\)

\begin{equation*} \int_x^{x+h} f(t) \, dt \approx f(x) \cdot h\text{,} \end{equation*}

by a simple left-hand approximation of the integral. Thus, as we take the limit in Equation (5.2.1), it follows that

\begin{equation*} A'(x) = \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{\int_x^{x+h} f(t) \, dt}{h} = \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{f(x) \cdot h}{h} = f(x)\text{.} \end{equation*}
