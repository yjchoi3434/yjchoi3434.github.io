Writing \(p(x) = x^3 - ax^2 = x^2(x-a)\text{,}\) we see that \(p(x) = 0\) at \(x=0\) and \(x=a\text{.}\) Further, since \(x^2 \ge 0\) for all \(x\text{,}\) we note that \(p(x) \le 0\) whenever \(x \lt a \) and \(p(x) \gt 0\) for all \(x \gt a\text{.}\) A typical such function is plotted below.

  2. To find the critical numbers of \(p\text{,}\) we first compute \(p'(x)\text{.}\) Treating \(a\) as constant, we have \(p'(x) = 3x^2 - 2ax = x(3x - 2a)\text{.}\) Thus, setting \(p'(x) = 0\text{,}\) we find that \(x(3x-2a) = 0\) and thus \(x = 0\) and \(x = \frac{2a}{3}\) are the critical numbers of \(p\text{.}\)

  3. Observe that \(p''(x) = 6x - 2a\) and thus \(p''(x) = 0\) implies that \(x = \frac{a}{3}\text{.}\) Since \(p''(x)\) is linear with positive slope, we see that \(p''(x)\) changes sign from negative to positive at \(x = \frac{a}{3}\text{,}\) and thus \(p\) is concave down for \(x \lt \frac{a}{3}\) and concave up for \(x \gt \frac{a}{3}\) and \(x = \frac{a}{3}\) is the only inflection point of \(p\text{.}\)

  4. There is always a critical number of \(p\) at \(x = 0\text{.}\) But the other critical number at \(x = \frac{2a}{3}\) and the inflection point at \(x = \frac{a}{3}\) both obviously depend directly on \(a\text{:}\) as we increase the value of \(a\text{,}\) both the location of the critical number and the inflection point move to the right along with \(a\text{.}\)
